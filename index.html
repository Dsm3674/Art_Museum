In the summer of 1937, two exhibitions opened within miles of each other in Munich. One was a gleaming showcase of classical Germanic painting — heroic, obedient, technically polished. The other was a deliberately humiliating display of what the Nazi regime called Entartete Kunst: Degenerate Art.

The regime pulled works from Expressionism, Dada, Surrealism, and New Objectivity — movements that had made Germany the center of the modern art world. They hung them crookedly, scrawled mocking captions on the walls, and placed anti-Semitic slurs beside portraits painted by Jewish artists. The intent was humiliation. The effect was consecration.

What the Nazis intended as a funeral became the last great rally of modern art in Europe. Two million people came to see it — three times the attendance of the official Great German Art Exhibition next door. Whether they came to mock or to mourn, no one knows. But they came, and the works burned with an authority that no amount of contempt could extinguish.

This is the story of the art that terrified a dictator. Of the painters who made it. Of what happened to them. And of the long aftermath — the German Blue Period, the restitutions, the still-unresolved losses — that followed when the regime finally collapsed, leaving behind wreckage it took generations to begin to account for.

The Dictator as Artist

The Man Who Failed Art School

In October 1907, an eighteen-year-old from Braunau am Inn presented himself at the Vienna Academy of Fine Arts for the entrance examination. He submitted drawings of buildings and a small selection of figure studies. The academy rejected him. His draughtsmanship was considered adequate, but his figures were lifeless. He lacked the essential gift.

He returned the following year and was rejected again — this time not even invited to the formal examination. The director who reviewed his portfolio suggested he might consider a career in architecture. Architecture, however, required a secondary school diploma he did not have. The door closed. He went to live in a men’s hostel in Vienna and began selling postcard-sized watercolors of city scenes to tourists and frame shops.

Adolf Hitler painted for nearly a decade, producing several hundred works. They are technically competent and completely empty. Buildings are rendered with photographic accuracy and zero feeling. His few human figures are awkward, flattened, incapable of depicting inner life. He had the hand of a draughtsman and the eye of a bureaucrat. He could copy what he saw. He could not transform it.

This failure shaped everything that followed. When Hitler came to power in 1933, his first substantial cultural act was the creation of the Reich Chamber of Culture — a totalitarian control apparatus for every art form in Germany. When he curated the Degenerate Art exhibition in 1937, he was doing so as a failed artist who had spent thirty years watching the men he envied receive the recognition he was denied. The exhibition was, among other things, an act of revenge.

The paintings he called degenerate had what his own work lacked: interiority, risk, the capacity to disturb. Kirchner’s figures were distorted because they were felt from the inside. Nolde’s colors were violent because he was painting spiritual states, not facades. Beckmann’s allegories were obscure because they were grappling with something real. Hitler recognized this quality and despised it. He could not produce it. He could only destroy it.

The Academy’s verdict on Hitler’s 1907 portfolio read: “Test drawing unsatisfactory. Few heads.” The following year he was rejected without interview. The registrar noted: “The applicant has talent for architecture but not for the fine arts. He should consider redirecting.” He did not redirect.

Chapter I

The Shame
Exhibition of 1937

The Ideological Framework

Hitler had a vision of art that was uncompromising and totalitarian. Beauty meant blood and soil — the muscled peasant, the noble warrior, the serene German mother. Cubist fragmentation was Bolshevist chaos. Expressionist distortion was racial degeneracy. Abstract work was a Jewish conspiracy against German feeling.

The concept of Entartung — degeneracy — had been circulating in pseudo-scientific discourse since the 1890s, borrowed from the criminologist Max Nordau. The Nazis weaponized it with systematic bureaucratic efficiency. In 1933, the Reich Chamber of Culture was formed. Artists had to join to practice their profession. Jewish artists were barred. Non-Jewish artists deemed “culturally Bolshevist” were expelled, forbidden to buy canvas, forbidden to paint.

Some were visited by the Gestapo. Officials arrived unannounced to inspect studios for evidence of continued work. The smell of fresh turpentine could mean arrest. Emil Nolde, despite being a Nazi Party member himself, had his studio sealed and was banned from all artistic activity. The bureaucracy of prohibition was total and petty, as such bureaucracies always are.

The Exhibition Itself

Adolf Ziegler, President of the Reich Chamber of Visual Arts, toured Germany in spring 1937 with a commission, pulling paintings and sculptures from museum walls with little documentation and sometimes no receipt. The museums had no recourse. Works by Kirchner, Nolde, Marc, Beckmann, Dix, Klee, Kandinsky, Kokoschka and hundreds more were seized and transported to Munich.

The Hofgarten arcades were chosen — cramped, poorly lit rooms that could barely contain the 650 works crammed into them. This was deliberate. Scrawled commentary was painted directly on the walls: “An insult to German womanhood,” “Paid for by the German taxpayer,” “Nature as seen by sick minds.” Mock psychiatric labels hung beside paintings of genuine spiritual depth.

What the Nazis did not anticipate was how powerful the art looked even under ridicule. The works spoke for themselves. Even compressed, even mocked, they burned with an authority that the polished mediocrity of the Great German Art Exhibition next door could not approach. By the time it closed in 1941, over three million people had seen it — the most-attended exhibition of modern art in history.

What Hitler Approved:
The Great German Art Exhibition

On the same day the Degenerate Art exhibition opened — July 19, 1937 — the Great German Art Exhibition opened a short walk away in the newly built Haus der Deutschen Kunst. It was the first major exhibition of Nazi-approved art and Hitler’s vision of what German culture could be: heroic, classical, technically proficient, and utterly hollow.

The works showed muscular farmers harvesting wheat, serene Aryan mothers with blond children, heroic soldiers and noble athletes. The aesthetic was a regression to 19th-century academic painting — smoothly rendered surfaces, correct anatomy, idealized subjects. Everything that art had learned about the interior life in the preceding fifty years was deliberately discarded.

The artists who dominated the exhibition — Adolf Ziegler, Arno Breker, Josef Thorak — were well-compensated, well-housed, and completely forgotten within a decade of the regime’s collapse. Their work did not survive the ideology that commissioned it. The art they mocked, the art they hung in a humiliation chamber two streets away, is now in the permanent collections of the greatest museums in the world.

This is the fundamental verdict of history on the aesthetics of authoritarianism: it cannot produce work that outlives its political moment. Art made in service of power decays with that power. Art made in defiance of power accumulates meaning as the years pass. The Degenerate Artists, painted, driven into exile, stripped of their livelihoods and in some cases their lives, won. The great machinery of Nazi culture production produced nothing that anyone needs to look at again.

Chapter II

The Artists
& Their Fates

Each name carries a different story — exile, silence, survival, resistance, death. These are the painters who made the art that terrified a regime.

01
Ernst Ludwig Kirchner1880 – 1938 · Expressionism
Founder of Die Brücke, Kirchner invented a new visual language — angular, electric, sexually charged. His Berlin street scenes captured the feverish modernity of Weimar Germany. Traumatized by WWI service, he spent his final years in a Swiss sanatorium. When the Nazis confiscated 639 of his works, the humiliation destroyed what remained of his stability. On June 15, 1938, he walked into the mountains near Davos and shot himself.

02
Max Beckmann1884 – 1950 · New Objectivity
On the very evening the Degenerate Art exhibition opened — July 19, 1937 — Beckmann listened to Hitler’s speech on the radio, packed two suitcases, and boarded a train to Amsterdam. He spent ten years in a cramped apartment painting enormous triptychs that encoded the horror of his age in mythological allegory. He never returned to Germany. He died in New York in 1950, on his way to view his own painting at the Metropolitan Museum.

03
Emil Nolde1867 – 1956 · Expressionism
The cruelest irony: Nolde was himself a Nazi Party member who had written to the regime praising its mission. Yet his art was too strange for the state. 1,052 of his works were confiscated. Banned from painting, he secretly produced over a thousand small watercolors hidden in his house — works he called “unpainted pictures” because they could never be shown. They are among the most beautiful works of the 20th century, made in defiance by a man who supported his own oppressors.

04
Otto Dix1891 – 1969 · New Objectivity
The most searing visual chronicler of WWI, Dix painted the trenches and mutilated veterans with a clinical ferocity the Nazis found intolerable. 260 of his works were confiscated. He retreated to the German-Swiss border and turned, under surveillance, to landscape painting — hiding his real satirical portraits in the attic. He survived. After 1945 he resumed figurative work, never explaining what he had seen. He died in 1969, old enough to be celebrated by the country that had tried to erase him.

05
Paul Klee1879 – 1940 · Bauhaus
Dismissed from his Düsseldorf Academy position in 1933, Klee fled to Bern. 102 of his works appeared in the Degenerate Art exhibition. In Swiss exile, he developed scleroderma, a disease that progressively paralyzed his hands. He responded by simplifying his line, producing the enormous childlike angels and stick figures of his late period with a body failing him — works of devastating grace made at the very end. He died in Muralto, Switzerland, June 29, 1940.

06
Franz Marc1880 – 1916 · Der Blaue Reiter
Marc did not live to see the Nazis. He was killed at Verdun in 1916 by a shell fragment while sketching a landscape. His art — in which animals carried a spiritual purity humans had lost — was exhibited posthumously in the Degenerate Art show. He believed blue was masculine and spiritual, yellow feminine and gentle, red violent and brutal. He was right about the red. His horse paintings, painted in belief, hung in a humiliation chamber sixteen years after his death.

07
Wassily Kandinsky1866 – 1944 · Abstraction
The inventor of pure abstraction fled Germany in 1933, the day the Bauhaus was closed. He settled in Neuilly-sur-Seine near Paris and continued painting until his death in 1944, one year before liberation. His theory that abstract form could carry the spiritual force of music was the very thing the Nazis most feared: art that communicated something real without depicting anything literal, without race, without soil, without triumph. He died in his apartment on December 13, 1944, working to the end.

Oskar Kokoschka1886 – 1980 · Expressionism
Declared “Kulturbolschewist Nr. 1” — Cultural Bolshevist Number One — by the Nazi press. He responded by painting a self-portrait titled “Degenerate Artist,” wearing the label as a badge. He fled to London in 1938, settled eventually in Switzerland, and lived until 1980 — to the age of 93 — outlasting every person who had ever called his work degenerate. He made sure to say so, often and loudly, for the rest of his long life.

Grosz emigrated to New York in January 1933, the same month Hitler became Chancellor, as though he had been watching the calendar. His savage caricatures of Weimar Germany’s decadence and militarism had made him one of the most despised artists in the Nazi imagination. 285 of his works were confiscated. In New York he taught at the Art Students League and tried, with only partial success, to reinvent himself as an American painter. He died in Berlin in 1959, having returned just months before.

László Moholy-Nagy1895 – 1946 · Bauhaus
Moholy-Nagy left Germany in 1933 when the Bauhaus closed, first to Amsterdam, then London, then Chicago, where he founded the New Bauhaus in 1937 — the same year the Degenerate Art show opened. He brought with him the Bauhaus philosophy of integrating fine art, design, and technology, planting it in American soil where it would eventually reshape graphic design, typography, and industrial design across the 20th century. He died of leukemia in Chicago in 1946, too young and too busy to know what he had built.

Chapter III

The Bauhaus:
What They Closed

1919 – 1925
Weimar

Walter Gropius founded the Bauhaus in Weimar in 1919 with a radical manifesto: unite all the arts under the roof of architecture. The school brought together the most extraordinary concentration of creative minds in Europe — Klee, Kandinsky, Itten, Feininger, Schlemmer, Moholy-Nagy. They reimagined typography, furniture, theater, weaving, photography, and graphic design simultaneously. The state government of Thuringia, growing hostile to what they called Bolshevist art instruction, forced the school to leave in 1925.

1925 – 1932
Dessau

In Dessau, Gropius built the school’s most famous structure — the Bauhaus building itself, with its glass curtain wall, its workshop wing, its masters’ houses in the pine forest outside town. It became the model for every modernist institutional building of the 20th century. Here Breuer designed the cantilever chair, Herbert Bayer systematized typography, Marcel Breuer invented a new way of sitting. The Nazi-allied government of Anhalt forced the school to close in 1932.

1932 – 1933
Berlin

Under Ludwig Mies van der Rohe, the Bauhaus reopened as a private institution in a former telephone factory in Berlin. It lasted eight months. The Gestapo raided the school in April 1933. Mies negotiated with the SS for weeks, trying to find terms under which the school could continue. There were none. The faculty voted to dissolve the institution on July 20, 1933. The masters scattered across Europe and America, carrying the Bauhaus idea with them like seeds in their pockets.

Degenerate Music:
What They Silenced

In May 1938, a parallel exhibition opened in Düsseldorf: Entartete Musik — Degenerate Music. Its poster showed a caricature of a Black jazz musician wearing a Star of David. The exhibition attacked jazz, atonality, and any music that deviated from the diatonic, the triadic, the comfortably German. It was, in miniature, the same ideology that had produced the Degenerate Art exhibition the year before.

The Nazis banned jazz in Germany in 1935, classifying it as “negro music” corrupted by Jewish composers and performers. Jazz clubs were closed. Radio stations were forbidden to broadcast swing. The ban was imperfectly enforced — Wehrmacht soldiers were known to dance to it in occupied France — but the intent was total suppression of anything that felt alive, improvisatory, sexually present, racially mixed.

Arnold Schönberg’s twelve-tone method was classified as Jewish-Bolshevist subversion. Kurt Weill’s theater music — the greatest popular-art fusion of the century — was banned entirely. Paul Hindemith was expelled from his professorship. Hanns Eisler, who had collaborated with Bertolt Brecht, fled to Hollywood. The effect was the same as in the visual arts: Germany lost its most interesting musical minds in a decade, and the music that replaced them was forgotten within a generation.

What the regime could not ban entirely was performed in secret. Cabaret continued underground. Jazz was played in private apartments. The Comedian Harmonists, a wildly popular close-harmony group, were forced to dissolve because three of their six members were Jewish — but their recordings continued to circulate in defiance, passed hand to hand like contraband.

Works Lost to Fire

On the morning of March 20, 1939, trucks drove to the yard of the Berlin fire brigade on Königstraße. Crates were unloaded. The fire was lit. What exactly burned has never been fully established — no inventory was kept, no photographs taken, no witnesses permitted. What follows is what we know was destroyed, drawn from confiscation records, dealer accounts, and the silence of museum walls where works once hung.

Chapter IV

The German
Blue Period

01
The Color of Mourning

After 1945, German art turned inward. The survivors returned to studios of silence. Across the work of artists processing the Trümmerzeit — the rubble years — critics identified an overwhelming turn toward blue: not the jubilant blue of Franz Marc’s horses, but a deeper, colder, more searching blue. The color of a country trying to see itself in the dark without flinching from what it would find there.

02
Baumeister & Reconstruction

Baumeister survived the Nazi years working as a paint factory technician, producing abstract work in total secrecy. After 1945 he returned to the Stuttgart Academy. His 1947 book The Unknown in Art argued that abstract art was the fundamental language of human inner life — a direct refutation, point by point, of twelve years of state aesthetics designed to crush exactly this claim.

03
ZERO & Tabula Rasa

In 1958, Heinz Mack, Otto Piene, and Günther Uecker formed ZERO in Düsseldorf. The name was deliberate: zero hour, ground zero, the point from which counting begins again. They renounced expressionism as contaminated by trauma. They worked with light, vibration, fire, aluminum grids. Their blue monochromes were proposals for a new kind of seeing — not forgetting, but beginning from the only honest place left: nothing.

04
Gerhard Richter’s Gray

Richter fled East Germany in 1961, the year the Wall was built. In West Germany he developed photo-painting — images dragged into blur with a squeegee — that became a visual metaphor for Germany’s relationship with its own history: present but smeared, visible but unresolvable. His Atlas project is an endless accumulation of images approaching the unspeakable indirectly, in fragments, never arriving at a verdict because no verdict is available.

05
Joseph Beuys & The Wound

Beuys mythologized his own wartime trauma into a new kind of art. His performances, his suits of felt, his rooms full of fat wedges addressed the body’s vulnerability and the possibility of healing. He called it social sculpture. He said everyone is an artist. He was the strangest and most necessary German artist of the postwar era, turning catastrophe into a kind of medicine, insisting that healing was possible even if he could not entirely perform it himself.

06
Anselm Kiefer & Memory

Kiefer is the culmination of postwar German art’s long reckoning with its own history. His vast canvases — charred, sand-encrusted, laced with lead and straw — depict landscapes of German mythology and German crime simultaneously. He excavated the Wagnerian imagery the Nazis had appropriated and burned it out, returning it as ash. His work proves that art can confront the darkest corners of history without looking away, and that the confrontation is not in vain.

The Burning and What Survived

On March 20, 1939, trucks arrived at the yard of the Berlin fire brigade. Crates were unloaded. The fire was lit. The exact count is disputed — some records say 4,000, some say 5,000. No inventory was kept. Kirchner’s oils, Nolde’s watercolors, Dix’s portraits, Klee’s drawings turned to smoke over a city that would itself be ash within six years. It was a deliberate act of erasure. They believed if you burned the object you destroyed the idea. They were wrong.

But not everything burned. The regime had also sold works on the international market through dealers in Lucerne, raising foreign currency. On June 30, 1939, a Galerie Fischer auction offered 126 confiscated works to foreign buyers. The Kunstmuseum Basel, the Museum of Modern Art in New York, and private collectors purchased them. The regime’s hatred funded the preservation of what it despised. The works entered the world’s greatest collections, where they remain today as the permanent rebuke their makers would have wanted.

Further works were stolen by individual Nazi officials for personal collections. Hermann Göring maintained his own cache of “degenerate” works at Carinhall, trading some on the black market, keeping others for private pleasure. The bureaucracy of theft and the bureaucracy of killing ran in parallel, staffed by the same people, for twelve years.

Chapter VI

The Long Work of Return

01
The Gurlitt Hoard

In 2012, German customs agents discovered 1,406 works of art in the Munich apartment of Cornelius Gurlitt, son of the Nazi art dealer Hildebrand Gurlitt. The cache included Picassos, Matisses, and major Expressionist works. Gurlitt died in 2014, bequeathing his collection to the Kunstmuseum Bern. Restitution negotiations continue for dozens of individual works.

02
Klimt & Maria Altmann

Maria Altmann, niece of Adele Bloch-Bauer, pursued the Austrian government for five Klimt paintings her family owned before the Anschluss. After a landmark U.S. Supreme Court ruling in 2004, Austria submitted to arbitration and returned all five paintings in 2006. Adele Bloch-Bauer I sold at Christie’s for $135 million — then a record for any painting at auction.

03
The Welfenschatz

Heirs of the Jewish art dealers who sold the medieval Guelph Treasure under duress in 1935 filed suit against the Prussian Cultural Heritage Foundation. The German Advisory Commission declined restitution. The heirs took the case to American courts. In 2021 the U.S. Supreme Court allowed the suit to proceed, marking a landmark in extraterritorial restitution law.

04
Schiele at the MoMA

In 1998, two drawings by Egon Schiele loaned by the Leopold Museum to the Museum of Modern Art in New York were seized by Manhattan District Attorney Robert Morgenthau as stolen property of the Lea Bondi Jaray estate. After years of legal dispute they were returned. The case helped establish international restitution precedent.

05
The Looted Libraries

Beyond visual art, the Nazis confiscated millions of books from Jewish libraries and private collections. The YIVO Institute alone lost hundreds of thousands of documents. Many are in the collections of national libraries across Europe. The work of identifying, cataloguing, and returning archival material remains decades behind the work done on paintings.

06
What Remains Unresolved

Estimates suggest 600,000 or more objects looted during the Nazi era have never been accounted for. Many institutions have been slow to conduct provenance research. The generation of survivors who could identify family heirlooms is dying. The window of living memory closes, slowly, every year.

Where They Hang Now

The Degenerate Works
in the World’s Museums

The paintings the Nazis mocked now occupy the permanent collections of the greatest museums on earth.

Museum of Modern Art
New York, USA
Street, Berlin — Kirchner, 1913. Purchased at the Lucerne auction, 1939.
Departure (Triptych) — Beckmann, 1932–35. Given by Beckmann himself before his death.
Composition VIII — Kandinsky, 1923. Gift of the Solomon R. Guggenheim Foundation, 1937.
Large Blue Horses — Franz Marc, on extended loan from Walker Art Center.
Neue Nationalgalerie
Berlin, Germany
Metropolis — Otto Dix, 1928. The most searing image of Weimar excess, now home in the city that inspired it.
Winter Landscape — Ernst Ludwig Kirchner, 1919. Returned from a Swiss collection.
The Sailor — Erich Heckel, 1913. Confiscated, survived the war in private storage, restituted 1998.
Kunstmuseum Basel
Basel, Switzerland
Woman at Her Toilette — Max Beckmann. Purchased at the Lucerne auction, 1939, before the burning.
Five Women — Ernst Ludwig Kirchner, 1913. Among the earliest major Kirchner acquisitions.
First museum in the world to systematically collect works from the Lucerne sale, acting in 1939 to preserve what the Nazis were selling off.
Tate Modern
London, UK
The Bride of the Wind — Oskar Kokoschka, 1913–14. One of the defining works of European Expressionism.
Danae — Kokoschka. Acquired following the artist’s long residence in England.
Tate holds the largest collection of Kokoschka works outside Austria — a direct consequence of his English exile years.
Solomon R. Guggenheim
New York, USA
Composition VIII — Kandinsky, 1923. Hilla von Rebay, a German-American artist, began acquiring Kandinsky for Solomon Guggenheim in 1930, inadvertently preserving works the Nazis would soon begin confiscating.
The Guggenheim holds more Kandinsky works than any other museum in the world.
Pinakothek der Moderne
Munich, Germany
In the same city where the Degenerate Art exhibition opened in 1937, the permanent collection now holds major works by Kirchner, Nolde, Beckmann, Dix, and Klee.
The proximity is deliberate. The museum stands as a direct institutional rebuke to the exhibition that opened a kilometre away eighty-seven years ago.

Legacy

Art
Survives
Everything

The Nazis burned the art and buried the artists. They built a thousand-year Reich and it lasted twelve. The paintings they mocked hang in the greatest museums on earth. The artists they silenced are studied in every academy. The regime that claimed to speak for civilization is remembered as its low-water mark. The art it called degenerate is what we call modern. History made its verdict. The brush outlasted the boot.

On the permanence of art

Every work that survived does so as a rebuke to the certainty of those who wanted it destroyed. Nolde’s hidden watercolors. Kirchner’s canvases in American museums. Beckmann’s triptychs painted in a Dutch apartment in terror. The permanence of these works is not an accident. It is a consequence of their quality — of the fact that they were made with everything their makers had, in defiance of everything that wanted them silent.

What degenerate art tells us

The Degenerate Art exhibition was, in its way, the Nazi regime’s most accurate self-portrait. In the art they chose to mock — the distorted faces, the desperate colors, the fractured geometries — they chose the art that most honestly depicted the world they were about to make. They called it sick. They were showing you a mirror. The artists they condemned understood the century. The regime that condemned them did not survive it.

The unfinished work

The legal and moral work of restitution continues. Every year, objects are identified, negotiations open, families are found or not found. Behind each case is a family that was destroyed, a collection assembled over generations, a life’s worth of aesthetic choices made by people who loved beautiful things and were killed for other reasons. The return of a painting cannot restore what was taken. But it can insist, in material form, that it happened, and that it was wrong.
